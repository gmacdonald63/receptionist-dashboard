# HVAC Industry Knowledge Base for AI Receptionist

## Common HVAC Systems and Equipment Types

### Central Air Conditioning (Split Systems)
A split system is the most common type of residential HVAC. It has two main components: an outdoor unit (condenser) and an indoor unit (evaporator coil and air handler). They are connected by refrigerant lines and use ductwork to distribute air throughout the home. Most homes in the U.S. use split systems. Typical lifespan is 15 to 20 years with proper maintenance.

### Heat Pumps
A heat pump works like an air conditioner in summer but reverses the process in winter to heat the home. It moves heat rather than generating it, making it more energy efficient than a traditional furnace. Heat pumps are ideal for moderate climates. In extremely cold climates, a dual-fuel or hybrid system that pairs a heat pump with a gas furnace backup is recommended. Common types include air-source heat pumps and ground-source (geothermal) heat pumps.

### Ductless Mini-Split Systems
Ductless mini-splits have an outdoor compressor and one or more indoor air-handling units mounted on walls. They do not require ductwork, making them ideal for room additions, converted garages, older homes without ducts, and spaces where extending ductwork is impractical. Each indoor unit has its own thermostat, allowing zoned temperature control.

### Packaged Units
A packaged unit contains all components (compressor, condenser, evaporator) in a single outdoor cabinet. These are commonly found in commercial buildings and some homes with limited indoor space. They can be packaged air conditioners, packaged heat pumps, or packaged gas-electric units.

### Furnaces
A furnace heats air and distributes it through ductwork. Gas furnaces are the most common, using natural gas or propane. Electric furnaces and oil furnaces also exist. Furnace efficiency is measured by AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency). Modern high-efficiency furnaces have AFUE ratings of 90% to 98.5%. A standard furnace has an AFUE of 80%. Typical lifespan is 15 to 30 years.

### Boilers
Boilers heat water and distribute steam or hot water through radiators or radiant floor systems. They do not use ductwork. Common fuel types are natural gas, oil, propane, and electricity. Boilers are more common in older homes and in northern regions of the U.S.

## HVAC Efficiency Ratings

### SEER and SEER2 (Cooling Efficiency)
SEER stands for Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio. It measures how efficiently an air conditioner or heat pump cools. Higher SEER means better efficiency and lower energy bills. As of January 2023, new residential AC units must meet minimum SEER2 ratings: 14.3 SEER2 in the northern U.S. and 15 SEER2 in the southern U.S. High-efficiency systems can have SEER2 ratings of 20 or higher.

### AFUE (Heating Efficiency for Furnaces)
AFUE stands for Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency. It measures what percentage of fuel a furnace converts to heat. A 96% AFUE furnace converts 96 cents of every dollar of fuel into heat. The minimum AFUE for new gas furnaces is 80%, but many modern furnaces are 90% to 98.5%.

### HSPF and HSPF2 (Heat Pump Heating Efficiency)
HSPF stands for Heating Seasonal Performance Factor. It measures how efficiently a heat pump heats. Higher HSPF means more efficient heating. Minimum is currently 7.5 HSPF2 for new heat pumps.

## Common Customer Problems and Troubleshooting

### AC Not Cooling or Not Blowing Cold Air
This is one of the most common summer calls. Possible causes include a dirty air filter restricting airflow, low refrigerant due to a leak, a frozen evaporator coil, a faulty compressor, a bad capacitor, a malfunctioning thermostat, or a tripped breaker. Questions to ask the caller: Is the system running at all or is it completely off? Is air blowing from the vents but it is just not cold? What is the thermostat set to and what temperature is it reading? When did you last change the air filter? Have you checked the circuit breaker?

### Heater or Furnace Not Working
Common in fall and winter. Possible causes include a tripped breaker or blown fuse, a dirty or clogged filter, thermostat set incorrectly, pilot light out (on older furnaces), ignition failure, a faulty flame sensor, a cracked heat exchanger, or a malfunctioning blower motor. Questions to ask: Is the thermostat set to heat mode? What temperature is it set to? Is the fan blowing any air at all? Do you hear the system trying to start? When was the last time the system was serviced? Have you checked the breaker?

### Strange Noises from the HVAC System
Banging or clanking usually indicates a loose or broken part inside the compressor or blower assembly. Squealing or screeching may be a worn belt or motor bearing issue. Buzzing may indicate an electrical issue, a failing contactor, or loose parts. Clicking at startup and shutdown is normal, but constant clicking can indicate a faulty relay or control board. Rattling may be loose screws, debris in the outdoor unit, or loose ductwork. Hissing may indicate a refrigerant leak.

### Thermostat Issues
The thermostat is not responding, is showing a blank screen, or temperature readings seem inaccurate. Common causes include dead batteries, loose wiring, a tripped breaker, a thermostat that needs recalibration, or an outdated thermostat that is incompatible with the HVAC system. Ask the caller: Is the screen blank or is it showing something? Have you tried replacing the batteries? What type of thermostat do you have (programmable, smart, manual)?

### Water Leaks Around the HVAC Unit
Water around the indoor unit may indicate a clogged condensate drain line, a cracked or overflowing drain pan, a frozen evaporator coil that is thawing, or a disconnected drain line. Ask: Where exactly is the water? How much water is there? Is the system still running? Is the unit blowing cold air?

### Frozen Evaporator Coil or Ice on the Unit
Ice on the indoor coil or refrigerant lines is usually caused by restricted airflow from a dirty filter, low refrigerant, a malfunctioning blower fan, or dirty evaporator coils. Advise the caller to turn the system off and switch the fan to ON to help thaw the ice, then schedule a service visit.

### Bad or Unusual Smells from Vents
A musty or moldy smell usually means mold or mildew in the ductwork or on the evaporator coil. A burning smell when first turning on the heat in fall is often just dust burning off and is usually normal, but a persistent burning smell could indicate an electrical issue. A rotten egg smell near a gas furnace is a potential gas leak and should be treated as an emergency.

### Uneven Temperatures Between Rooms
Some rooms are too hot while others are too cold. Common causes include leaky ductwork, blocked or closed vents, poor insulation, an improperly sized system, or duct design issues. Zoning systems or ductless mini-splits can help solve this.

### High Energy Bills
A sudden spike in energy costs often points to a dirty filter, a refrigerant leak, an aging or failing system, thermostat issues, ductwork leaks, or lack of maintenance. Regular tune-ups help keep energy costs down.

### Short Cycling
The system turns on and off frequently in short bursts. This can be caused by an oversized system, a dirty filter, a malfunctioning thermostat, refrigerant issues, or an overheating furnace. This puts extra wear on the equipment and should be addressed.

## Emergency vs. Routine Call Classification

### Emergency Situations (Immediate Attention Required)
- Gas smell or suspected gas leak near the furnace or gas lines. Instruct the caller to leave the home immediately, do not flip any switches, and call the gas company or 911.
- Carbon monoxide alarm going off. Instruct the caller to evacuate immediately and call 911.
- Burning electrical smell that is persistent and strong.
- Complete heating failure when outdoor temperatures are below freezing, especially if elderly, infants, or medically vulnerable people are in the home.
- Complete AC failure during extreme heat (above 95 degrees) with vulnerable individuals in the home.
- Sparks, smoke, or visible electrical arcing from the HVAC system.
- Major water flooding from the HVAC system causing property damage.
- Loud banging or explosion-like sounds from the furnace.

### Urgent (Same-Day or Next-Day Service)
- AC not cooling during hot summer weather.
- Heater not working during cold weather (but not life-threatening).
- Water leak that is contained but ongoing.
- System making loud or unusual noises.
- Frozen coils or ice on the system.
- System running constantly without reaching set temperature.

### Routine (Schedule Within a Few Days)
- Uneven temperatures between rooms.
- Minor thermostat issues.
- Preventive maintenance or tune-up request.
- Air quality concerns (dusty, stale air).
- Questions about upgrading or replacing equipment.
- Requests for quotes on new installations.
- Filter replacement assistance.

## HVAC Services Explained

### Diagnostic or Service Call
A technician visits to assess and diagnose a specific problem. This typically involves inspecting the system, running tests, and identifying the root cause of the issue. A diagnostic fee usually applies and may or may not be applied toward the cost of repair if the customer proceeds with the fix.

### Repair
Fixing a specific component or issue that has been diagnosed. This could range from replacing a capacitor or contactor to fixing a refrigerant leak or replacing a blower motor. Repair costs vary depending on the part and labor involved.

### AC Tune-Up (Cooling Season Maintenance)
A preventive maintenance visit for the air conditioning system, typically done in spring before summer. A standard tune-up generally includes checking and cleaning the condenser coils, checking refrigerant levels, inspecting electrical connections, testing the thermostat, checking the condensate drain, inspecting the air filter, lubricating moving parts, and testing overall system performance. Regular tune-ups help prevent breakdowns, improve efficiency, and extend equipment life.

### Furnace or Heating Tune-Up (Heating Season Maintenance)
A preventive maintenance visit for the heating system, typically done in fall before winter. A standard heating tune-up generally includes inspecting the heat exchanger for cracks, checking the burner and ignition system, testing safety controls, inspecting the flue and venting, checking the air filter, testing the thermostat, and checking carbon monoxide levels. This is important for both efficiency and safety.

### Full System Installation or Replacement
Removing an old HVAC system and installing a new one. This is a major project that includes equipment selection and sizing, removal and disposal of old equipment, installation of new indoor and outdoor units, connecting refrigerant lines, electrical work, thermostat installation, ductwork modifications if needed, system testing and commissioning, and often includes permits and inspections. A typical residential installation takes 1 to 3 days depending on the complexity.

### Duct Cleaning
Professional cleaning of the ductwork to remove dust, debris, mold, and allergens. This is recommended every 3 to 5 years, or sooner if there are allergy concerns, visible mold, pest infestations, or excessive dust. Duct cleaning helps improve indoor air quality and system efficiency.

### Indoor Air Quality Services
Services that improve the air quality inside the home. This can include air purifier or filtration system installation, UV light systems that kill mold and bacteria, humidifier or dehumidifier installation, ventilation improvements, and air quality testing.

### Maintenance Plans or Service Agreements
Many HVAC companies offer annual maintenance plans or service agreements. These typically include one or two scheduled tune-ups per year (one for heating and one for cooling), discounts on repairs, priority scheduling, and sometimes waived diagnostic fees. Maintenance plans help customers stay on top of preventive care and can save money on repairs.

## Seasonal HVAC Information

### Spring
Spring is the ideal time to schedule an AC tune-up before the summer heat arrives. Homeowners should change their air filter, clear debris from around the outdoor unit, and test the AC to make sure it is working properly before they need it. Many HVAC companies run spring tune-up specials.

### Summer
Summer is peak season for AC repairs and installations. Common issues include AC not cooling, frozen coils, refrigerant leaks, and thermostat issues. Wait times for service may be longer during heat waves. Customers should keep their filters clean and avoid setting the thermostat extremely low, which strains the system.

### Fall
Fall is the time to schedule a furnace or heating tune-up before winter. Homeowners should test their heater early, replace the air filter, and check their carbon monoxide detectors. This is also a good time to consider upgrading an aging system before the winter rush.

### Winter
Winter is peak season for heating repairs. Common issues include furnaces not turning on, heat pumps struggling in extreme cold, pilot light problems, and frozen pipes affecting boiler systems. Emergency no-heat calls are common. Customers should never block vents with furniture, keep filters clean, and have carbon monoxide detectors working.

## Common Customer FAQ

### How often should I change my air filter?
Standard 1-inch filters should be changed every 1 to 3 months. Homes with pets, allergies, or high dust levels should change them monthly. Thicker 4-inch or 5-inch media filters can last 6 to 12 months. A dirty filter restricts airflow and makes the system work harder, leading to higher energy bills and potential breakdowns.

### How long does an HVAC system last?
Air conditioners and heat pumps typically last 15 to 20 years. Furnaces can last 15 to 30 years. Boilers can last 15 to 30 years or more. Ductless mini-splits typically last 15 to 20 years. Regular maintenance is the single biggest factor in extending equipment life.

### How much does a new HVAC system cost?
Pricing varies based on the size of the home, the type and efficiency of the equipment, ductwork needs, and the complexity of the installation. It is best to schedule an in-home consultation for an accurate quote. Many companies offer financing options to help with the investment.

### Do you offer financing?
Many HVAC companies offer financing options for major repairs and new installations. Ask about available plans, interest rates, and monthly payment options during your consultation.

### What size HVAC system do I need?
System sizing depends on the square footage of the home, insulation levels, number of windows, climate zone, and other factors. A professional load calculation (Manual J) is the proper way to determine the correct size. An oversized system short cycles and wastes energy. An undersized system runs constantly and cannot keep up.

### What is a SEER rating and why does it matter?
SEER stands for Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio. It measures cooling efficiency. A higher SEER rating means the system uses less energy to cool the home, resulting in lower utility bills. The current minimum for new systems is around 14 to 15 SEER2 depending on region. Higher efficiency models (18+ SEER2) cost more upfront but save money over time through lower energy bills.

### How often should I get my HVAC system serviced?
HVAC systems should be professionally serviced at least once a year. The best practice is twice a year: once in spring for the AC and once in fall for the heater. Regular maintenance catches small problems before they become expensive repairs, improves efficiency, and extends the life of the equipment.

### Can I do any HVAC maintenance myself?
Homeowners can regularly change or clean air filters, keep the area around the outdoor unit clear of debris, leaves, and vegetation (at least 2 feet clearance), keep supply and return vents open and unblocked, and test the thermostat periodically. However, refrigerant handling, electrical work, and internal component maintenance should always be done by a licensed HVAC technician.

### What is a heat exchanger and why is it important?
The heat exchanger is a metal component inside the furnace that heats the air. If a heat exchanger develops a crack, it can leak carbon monoxide into the home, which is dangerous. Cracked heat exchangers are a serious safety concern and typically require furnace replacement. This is one key reason why annual heating tune-ups are important.

### What temperature should I set my thermostat to?
For energy savings, the U.S. Department of Energy recommends 78 degrees in summer when home and 68 degrees in winter when home. Setting the thermostat back 7 to 10 degrees for 8 hours a day can save up to 10% on annual heating and cooling costs. Smart or programmable thermostats make this easy.

## Information the AI Receptionist Should Collect on Service Calls

### For All Calls
- Caller's full name
- Phone number
- Property address or service address
- Whether they are an existing customer or a new customer
- Best time for an appointment
- How they heard about the company (if a new customer)

### For Repair or Service Calls
- Description of the problem in their own words
- When the problem started
- Type of system if they know it (AC, furnace, heat pump, boiler, mini-split)
- Brand and approximate age of the equipment if known
- Is the system currently running or completely off?
- Has anything unusual happened (strange noises, smells, leaks)?
- Have they tried any troubleshooting (checked breaker, changed filter, checked thermostat)?
- Urgency level: is anyone in the home who is elderly, an infant, or medically vulnerable?

### For New Installation or Replacement Quotes
- Type of system they currently have
- Approximate age of the current system
- Reason for replacement (old system, frequent repairs, want better efficiency, renovation)
- Size of the home (approximate square footage)
- Number of stories
- Any specific preferences (brand, efficiency level, features)
- Budget range if they are willing to share
- Timeline for the project

### For Maintenance or Tune-Up Appointments
- Type of system (AC, furnace, both)
- When the system was last serviced
- Any current concerns or issues
- Whether they are interested in a maintenance plan

## HVAC Industry Terminology Quick Reference

### Common Terms Callers May Use or Ask About
- **Compressor**: The outdoor component that pumps refrigerant and is essential for cooling.
- **Condenser**: The outdoor unit that releases heat from the refrigerant.
- **Evaporator Coil**: The indoor coil that absorbs heat from the air to cool it.
- **Refrigerant (Freon)**: The chemical that absorbs and releases heat to cool the air. R-410A is the current standard. R-22 (Freon) has been phased out.
- **Blower Motor**: The motor that pushes air through the ductwork.
- **Capacitor**: An electrical component that helps start and run the compressor and fan motors.
- **Contactor**: An electrical switch that controls the flow of electricity to the compressor and condenser fan.
- **Thermostat**: The control device that sets and regulates the temperature.
- **Ductwork**: The system of channels that carries heated or cooled air throughout the building.
- **Return Air**: The air pulled back into the system from the home to be heated or cooled again.
- **Supply Air**: The conditioned air blown from the system into the home.
- **Air Handler**: The indoor unit that contains the blower and evaporator coil.
- **Condensate Drain**: The line that removes moisture collected by the AC system.
- **Heat Exchanger**: The furnace component that transfers heat to the air.
- **Zoning System**: A setup that allows different areas of the home to be heated or cooled to different temperatures independently.
- **Load Calculation (Manual J)**: The industry-standard method for calculating the correct HVAC system size for a building.
- **BTU (British Thermal Unit)**: A measurement of heating and cooling capacity. One ton of cooling equals 12,000 BTUs per hour.
- **Tonnage**: A measurement of cooling capacity. Residential systems are commonly 1.5 to 5 tons.
- **Variable Speed**: A blower or compressor that can operate at different speeds for better comfort and efficiency.
- **Two-Stage**: A system that can operate at high and low capacity, providing better comfort and efficiency than single-stage.
- **Smart Thermostat**: A WiFi-connected thermostat (like Nest, Ecobee, or Honeywell Home) that can be controlled remotely and learns usage patterns.

## R-22 Freon Phase-Out Information

R-22 refrigerant (commonly called Freon) was phased out of production in the United States as of January 1, 2020 due to its harmful effects on the ozone layer. Systems that still use R-22 can continue to operate, but the refrigerant is increasingly expensive and scarce. If an R-22 system develops a leak, it is often more cost-effective to replace the system with a new one that uses R-410A or the newer R-454B refrigerant rather than repairing and recharging with R-22. Customers with R-22 systems should be made aware that replacement may be the better long-term investment.
